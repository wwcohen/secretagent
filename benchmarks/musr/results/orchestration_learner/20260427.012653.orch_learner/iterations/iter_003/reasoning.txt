1. **Specific failure pattern identified:** The LLM's `deduce_murderer` function is performing zero-shot classification on complex mystery logic. Because it lacks a Chain-of-Thought (CoT) "scratchpad," it fails to logically integrate the structured alibi data and instead pattern-matches against the instruction "Weight physical evidence heavily." This causes it to jump to conclusions, often picking suspects with obvious but circumstantial connections to the weapon, even when their timelines are logically solid compared to the true murderer.

2. **How many failures share this pattern:** All 19 out of 19 failures show `deduce_murderer` outputting just a single name without any intermediate reasoning (indicated by the tiny $0.0005 cost and single-word output). 

3. **Minimal change that fixes this pattern:** I am modifying the `deduce_murderer` docstring to require step-by-step reasoning ("Think step-by-step...") before concluding. Crucially, I am also softening the strict physical evidence heuristic to require *timeline alignment* ("carefully ensure it aligns with a realistic timeline"). I am *not* modifying any Python workflow code or string parsers—I am trusting the existing `extract_index` from `ptools_common` to parse the final answer out of the generated paragraph.

4. **Why this change will not break currently working cases:** Iteration 2 proved that CoT vastly improves generalization on this dataset (eval jumped from 73.3% to 80.0%), but it was rolled back because modifying the Python parsing logic likely caused a brittle failure on the training set. By removing the custom Python parsing and relying entirely on the robust `ptools_common` `extract_index` function (which easily handles CoT outputs ending in "Final Answer: [Name]"), we capture the huge CoT logic gains safely, avoiding Iter 1's over-correction against red herrings and Iter 2's brittle workflow changes.
