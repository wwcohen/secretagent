1. **Specific failure pattern identified**: The model consistently fails when there are deep interpersonal conflicts between team members. Specifically, it often pairs two people who explicitly dislike, argue with, or blame each other into the same 2-person task (e.g., in `ex096`, `ex091`, `ex046`, `ex022`). It incorrectly prioritizes individual skill descriptions over fatal relationship constraints.
2. **How many failures share this pattern**: Nearly all of the 19 failures follow this exact structure: a 3-person team where one task requires 2 people and the other requires 1. In almost every failure, the correct answer requires isolating the person who conflicts with the others into the 1-person task, and grouping the synergistic pair in the 2-person task.
3. **Minimal change**: Define a new `@interface` function called `reason_and_allocate` that acts as a Chain-of-Thought (CoT) processor. The prompt explicitly instructs the LLM to identify conflicts/synergies *first* and establishes hard rules: NEVER pair conflicting people in the 2-person task, and isolate a toxic person (conflicts with both others) into the 1-person task. Then update both `answer_question` and `answer_question_orchestrated_seed` to use this new function instead of the zero-shot `raw_answer`.
4. **Why it won't break working cases**: This relies on the fundamental latent structure of the MUSR team allocation dataset. By asking for step-by-step reasoning that prioritizes conflict avoidance, we prevent the model from blindly matching keywords. Since we aren't hardcoding names or specific task strings, this change will cleanly generalize to the held-out evaluation set, directly answering the docstring's hint about the success of "simulating with thinking".
