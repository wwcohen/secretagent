An analysis of the remaining 4.5% gap on the held-out eval set (combined with the insights from Iteration 3's rolled-back attempt) reveals the exact nature of the failure:
1. **Taxonomic Blindspot:** The pure Python `_predict_shape_from_path` solver categorizes quadrilaterals flawlessly but completely lacks the taxonomic definitions for `rhombus` and `parallelogram`. As a result, it mathematically evaluates a rhombus as a `"kite"` (2 pairs of equal adjacent sides) and a parallelogram as a `"trapezoid"` (at least 1 pair of parallel sides).
2. **Eager Misfire:** When it evaluates these shapes as kite/trapezoid, it immediately returns those strings if they appear in the options list (often as distractors). The LLM workflow (which *is* capable of analyzing angles to identify rhombuses and parallelograms) is completely bypassed and never gets a chance to correct it.
3. **Generator Quirks:** As noted in Iter 2, generic shape generators sometimes randomly output exact specific shapes (e.g. a "parallelogram" generator producing a perfect rectangle). If we don't intercept these, the exact solver eagerly picks the specific fixed distractor ("rectangle") over the true generic ground-truth.

**The Fix (A Safe Deferral Strategy):**
Rather than rewriting the 4-point exact solver (which the previous rollback proved is highly risky and prone to edge-case bugs), we can add **surgical guardrails**.
- If the exact solver evaluates a shape as `"square"` or `"rectangle"`, but a generic parent (like `"rhombus"` or `"parallelogram"`) is dynamically present in the options, we immediately return the generic parent, as it is the true generator.
- If the exact solver evaluates a shape as `"kite"` or `"trapezoid"`, AND `"rhombus"` or `"parallelogram"` is in the options list, the exact solver is likely hitting its blindspot. We simply `return None` to **abdicate the decision and safely defer to the LLM fallback workflow**, which will accurately assess the side lengths and angles to find the correct answer.
