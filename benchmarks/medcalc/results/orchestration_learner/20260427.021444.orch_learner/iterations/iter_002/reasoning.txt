1. What SPECIFIC failure pattern did you identify?
The pipeline's accuracy dropped because the orchestrator LLM (via the `calculate_medical_value` entrypoint) completely bypassed the highly-accurate induced tools, attempting to answer complex calculations zero-shot. This led to hallucinated formulas and arithmetic errors (e.g., incorrectly summing points for HAS-BLED and APACHE II, or messing up the math for Delta Gap and MELD Na).

2. How many of the shown failures share this pattern?
Nearly all of them. Over 20 failures are clinical scores (APACHE II, HAS-BLED, GBS, Child-Pugh, Wells, HEART, PSI) and several others are complex formulas (Delta Gap, Free Water Deficit) which were handled perfectly by the tools during induction but failed here due to zero-shotting.

3. What is the MINIMAL change that fixes this pattern?
Instead of forcing a rigid pure Python router (which hurt accuracy in Iter 1), I improved the docstring of the `calculate_medical_value` entrypoint to strictly instruct the LLM orchestrator to delegate to the specialized tools. I also expanded the `apply_clinical_score` docstring to include precise reference criteria for the failing scoring systems, and re-introduced `compute_calculation_impl` as a direct Python math evaluator to give the LLM a way to prevent summation and arithmetic errors.

4. Why will this change NOT break currently working cases?
It preserves the LLM's natural flexibility as an orchestrator while steering it toward the specialized tools that previously achieved a high 66.4% accuracy during induction. The expanded criteria in `apply_clinical_score` serve purely as a reference guide, reducing hallucinations without forcing strict logic. The mathematical evaluator is provided as a safe, optional tool that avoids rigid routing failures.
