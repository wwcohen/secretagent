1. **Identify the specific failure pattern**: The model currently routes the task through `answer_question_workflow_orchestrated_seed` directly to `answer_question`. In turn, `answer_question` invokes `raw_answer(narrative, question, choices)` which is a purely zero-shot LLM call. Theory of mind (ToM) tasks intrinsically require state-tracking (e.g., initial state -> moves -> who was present/distracted). Zero-shot LLMs routinely misattribute movements and fail to account for distraction or absence, leading to "false belief" evaluation errors.
2. **How many of the shown failures share this pattern?**: All 33 failures show a zero-shot misinterpretation. Characters who are explicitly described as "engrossed", "in another room", or "on a call" miss an object moving, meaning their belief stays frozen. The zero-shot prompt ignores this and predicts the new location, or gets confused by distractor objects.
3. **What is the MINIMAL change that fixes this pattern?**: Introduce a dedicated `@interface` function called `reason_and_answer` that forces the LLM to execute a concise, step-by-step Chain of Thought (CoT). The CoT logic explicitly guides the LLM to track the object's movements and severely scrutinize whether the target person was "distracted/immersed/away" during the move. We then update `answer_question` to call this `reason_and_answer` function instead of the naive `raw_answer`.
4. **Why will this change NOT break currently working cases?**: This approach maintains a single-turn reasoning trace, avoiding the budget/cost/timeout blowouts that broke Iter 1 (which attempted a bulky 4-step pipeline). The final integer extraction logic (`extract_index`) is strictly preserved and merely handed a much cleaner, explicitly structured input text. The function signatures remain entirely unchanged.
