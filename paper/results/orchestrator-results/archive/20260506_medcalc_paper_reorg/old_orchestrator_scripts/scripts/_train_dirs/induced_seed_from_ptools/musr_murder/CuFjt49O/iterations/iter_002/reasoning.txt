1. What SPECIFIC failure pattern did you identify?
In the failure cases, the LLM hallucinates a '1' for innocent suspects. This happens for two reasons: 
a) It evaluates "ghost" suspects who are mentioned exactly once in the intro paragraph and hallucinates evidence against them.
b) For suspects that *are* investigated, the LLM gets distracted by red herrings (e.g., bad behavior, arguments, general motives) and misses the core deductive logic of the dataset, which relies on matching the suspect to the specific murder weapon.

2. How many of the shown failures share this pattern?
All 17 failures share this pattern. In every case, the true expected murderer has a clear, explicit connection to the exact murder weapon in the text (e.g., owns a sai, uses a hatchet for work, has bleach on their boat), while the innocent suspect either has zero mentions after the introduction or no connection to the weapon.

3. What is the MINIMAL change that fixes this pattern?
- **Python Guardrail:** Short-circuit the LLM call and automatically assign a score of 0 if a suspect is mentioned $\le 1$ time. A suspect mentioned only once cannot be the subject of deductive reasoning.
- **Precision Prompting:** Replace the vague "strong evidence" prompt injection with a highly structured instruction that explicitly forces the LLM to: (1) Identify the weapon from paragraph 1, (2) Look for a direct link between the suspect and that exact weapon, and (3) Ignore general motives or interpersonal conflicts.

4. Why will this change NOT break currently working cases?
The `count <= 1` check is mathematically conservative; it only bypasses the LLM for pure distractors, preserving the evaluation of legitimately investigated suspects. The refined prompt directly aligns with the fundamental deductive mechanism of the MuSR dataset (weapon linking), which will solidify true positives that previously worked while preventing false positives caused by red herrings.
